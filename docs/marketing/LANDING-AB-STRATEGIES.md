# Landing page A/B strategies — Contractor Ops (expanded)

Audience shorthand (from product positioning):

- **Who:** 10–200 person companies, **5–50 B2B contractors**, EU-first; **software, agencies, product orgs, consultancies, scaleups**.
- **Buyers:** Head of Finance/Ops, COO, sometimes Founder; **pains:** invoice chaos, approval latency, contract mismatch, audit anxiety, KSeF/e-invoicing pressure.
- **Wedge to prove:** **Invoice → contract match → approval → payment** with audit trail — not “full HRIS” and not “mini-Deel.”

Pick **one primary angle per URL or test cell**. The deep playbooks below include **slogan-level AND full narrative**; use the playbooks to brief design and copy, not only the hero.

---

## How to use A/B tests (recap)

| Test layer | What you split | What you keep stable |
|------------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Hero** | Headline, subhead, one visual | Same information architecture, or you confound the test |
| **Social proof** | Logos vs. quote vs. metrics | Same hero |
| **CTA** | Trial vs. demo vs. pricing | Same scroll depth to pricing |

**North-star metrics (pick 2–3):** qualified demo/trial, work-email sign-up, scroll to pricing, bounce on hero.

---

## What a “complete” landing story contains (all variants)

Regardless of angle, a strong B2B page usually walks this arc (order can shift slightly):

1. **Hero** — one clear job + CTA; no competing promises.
2. **Agitation or outcome** — either make the cost of the status quo felt (pain variants) or make the future state believable (outcome variants).
3. **Mechanism** — *how* your product does the job in plain language (not a feature list dump).
4. **Specific capabilities** — 3–5 bullets tied to the wedge (invoice, contract, approval, pay run, offboarding) as *outcomes*, not Jira tickets.
5. **Proof** — one strong form: logo bar, short quote, or concrete metric; segment-specific is better than generic.
6. **Who it’s for / not for** — reduces unqualified sign-ups; makes sophisticated buyers trust you.
7. **Objections** — pricing anxiety, “another tool,” security, KSeF/ERP, implementation time.
8. **Pricing or next step** — clear motion; if demo-led, say what happens in the first call.
9. **Footer trust** — security, DPA, company, contact; keeps RFP and finance buyers on the page.

The variants below differ mainly in **hero emphasis**, **agitation style**, and **which objections come first**—not in skipping the arc.

## Variant A — Pain-first: “Spreadsheet + email” chaos

### Strategy (summary)

Name the current stack as the enemy. The visitor should feel *seen* in the first five seconds, then **immediately** see a constructive path (week-one value), or the page feels like a rant.

### Visitor mindset

- **Jobs-to-be-done (emotional):** “I am not bad at my job; the process is held together with glue.”
- **Jobs-to-be-done (practical):** “Get invoices correct, approved, and paid without chasing people in three channels.”
- **Fear:** Adding software will create *more* work (migration, training, another login).
- **Skepticism:** “I have heard this before from tools that are really just a database.”

### Page narrative (recommended order)

1. **Hero** — chaos named; CTA: trial + “see how it works” scroll anchor.
2. **“This is your stack today”** — five icons (sheet, email, Slack, bank export, file storage) with one line each: what breaks. No competitor logos.
3. **“What changes in the first week”** — 3 steps: e.g. connect/import contractors → first invoice in workflow → first approval. Concrete.
4. **Mechanism** — one paragraph: *single place* where contract terms inform what finance expects, what approvers see, and what gets batched to pay. Tie to your real workflow names.
5. **Proof** — quote from “Head of Ops/Finance” about time reclaimed or “fewer back-and-forth emails.”
6. **“Who it’s for”** — 5–50 B2B contractors, EU; not payroll for employees, not EOR.
7. **Objections** — see below; FAQ optional.
8. **Pricing / trial** + footer trust.

### Hero — draft copy block

- **Eyebrow (optional):** For teams that outgrew Excel, not for teams that outgrew discipline.
- **Headline:** Stop running B2B contractors in spreadsheets, threads, and “who has the latest PDF?”
- **Subhead:** One place for contracts, contractor records, incoming invoices, approvals, and payment runs—built for EU companies with a handful to dozens of B2B contractors, not a global payroll product.
- **Primary CTA:** Start free trial  
- **Secondary CTA (link/ghost):** See how the workflow fits your team (scroll)  
- **Microcopy under CTAs (trust without legalism):** No credit card for trial *if true*; or “See pricing before you commit” linking to section.

**Visual direction:** Left: messy composite (stylized, not cringe) of overlapping windows—right: one clean “contractor → invoice → approval” card. If you can’t do tasteful “messy,” use **before/after** copy only + single product shot.

### Section copy (deeper than slogans)

**“Your stack was never a system” (agitation, short)**

Use 4–5 bullet **symptoms** visitors recognize—written as *their* day, not your product name in every line:

- Invoices land in the inbox; someone forwards them; the thread forks; finance gets a different version than the manager.
- The “approved amount” lives in a spreadsheet cell; the bank file was built on Tuesday; a Thursday invoice *looks* the same in email but is not the same in contract.
- When someone asks “who approved this?” the answer is a scavenger hunt.

**Transition line (one sentence):**  
Contractor Ops is the execution layer: the same object (contractor + contract + invoice) in front of everyone who must act.

**Week one (concrete, numbers optional)**

- Day 1–2: contractor and contract records in one place; stop duplicating data across sheets.
- First invoice: enters your approval path, not a DM.
- First payment run: batch what was approved, with a trail someone can read without opening mail.

(Adjust to match **actual** onboarding.)

### Objections to surface (and response angles)

| Objection | Response angle (copy, not feature dump) |
|-----------|----------------------------------------|
| We already have an ERP / accounting | You keep the **ledger**; we coordinate **contractor** intake, match, and approval *before* the entry hits accounting with drama. |
| KSeF / e-invoicing is a separate product | KSeF is a **channel**; the hard part is **process** and matching—show how you sit in the loop without replacing their accountant’s tool. |
| Another tool = more work | “Week one” must be *specific*; avoid “onboarding in minutes” unless you can defend it. |
| We’re not that big | Explicit band: 5–50 contractors; if smaller, be honest that you might not be the ICP. |

### Proof to collect from customers (for this variant)

- **Quote:** A sentence about *communication load* (Slack/email) before vs. after, ideally from **finance + manager**.
- **Metric (soft):** “X fewer tools” only if you can list them credibly in sales calls.

### Sub-tests within A

- Hero: **“threads”** vs **“inbox + spreadsheet”** (which image of chaos resonates).
- Agitation: **4 symptom bullets** vs **one short story paragraph** (150 words max).

## Variant B — Outcome-first: time, errors, predictability

### Strategy (summary)

Lead with a **credible** operational result. Outcome messaging fails when numbers look invented; it wins when tied to a **mechanism** the buyer already believes in (matching, single approval path, batching).

### Visitor mindset

- **Emotional:** “I need the board to stop asking why payments are late or weird.”
- **Practical:** “Reduce rework and ‘sorry, wrong account’ messages.”
- **Skepticism:** “80% time savings” triggers disbelief. Prefer **structural** claims: fewer handoffs, fewer states where invoice and contract disagree.

### Page narrative

1. **Hero** — outcome + one mechanism line (match invoice to contract *before* pay).
2. **“What goes wrong without a system”** — 3 failure modes: duplicate pay, pay against wrong terms, accrual vs. cash confusion (pick what you actually help with; don’t claim all).
3. **Mechanism** — approval graph in plain English; who sees what; what gets blocked.
4. **“What you stop doing”** — bullet list of tasks (re-keying, reconciling three spreadsheets, pinging for “final PDF”).
5. **Proof** — customer quote about **rework** or **month-end**; optional: “teams report” style stats with a footnote.
6. **ICP + not for** + pricing/trial.
7. **FAQ** — heavy on “integration with accounting” and “what you do not replace.”

### Hero — draft copy block

- **Headline (pick one tone):**  
  - *Concrete:* One place to match every contractor invoice to a contract before it goes to the bank.  
  - *Outcome-leaning:* Fewer wrong payments, fewer month-end fire drills—without replacing your bank or your accountant’s system.
- **Subhead:** Route invoices through the right approvers, batch what’s cleared, and keep a defensible record of who said yes to what. Built for B2B contractors in the EU, at SMB scale.
- **Primary CTA:** Start free trial  
- **Secondary:** See what gets automated (scroll)

**Defensibility line (small text under subhead or in footnote):**  
If you use quantified marketing (“50% less email”), add: *“Based on N customers; individual results vary”* or switch to *“Many teams see fewer manual steps per invoice”* without a number.

### Section copy (deeper)

**Failure modes (short subsection)**

- **Mismatch:** invoice amount or period doesn’t match active contract; without a system, that is caught late.
- **Shadow approvals:** a manager “approved” in Slack; finance never had the same number.
- **Batch integrity:** the upload to the bank was built from a file that is not the same as what approvers thought they approved.

**“What you automate” vs. “what you still own” (trust)**

- You **own** contract language, tax, and final accounting entries with your firm.
- We **orchestrate** the contractor object, the intake, the approval event, and the handoff to pay—so the **same** data drives action.

### Objections

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Prove the ROI | Offer **demo** with a walkthrough of one invoice’s journey; or a one-page “steps removed” PDF lead magnet, not a fake calculator. |
| We have approvals in the ERP | Often **too late** in the process or **not** at contractor object granularity—explain without trashing the ERP. |

### Proof to collect

- **Quote** mentioning **month-end** or “approval trail” in an audit.
- **Optional metric:** average approvers per invoice, or time from receipt to “ready to pay” *if* you can measure in product and aggregate ethically.

### Sub-tests within B

- **Mechanism-first headline** (match invoice to contract) vs **outcome-first** (fewer errors).
- **CTA:** trial vs. “Get the one-pager: invoice-to-pay checklist” (lead gen for longer cycles).

## Variant C — Compliance & e-invoicing (KSeF / EU)

### Strategy (summary)

Regulation is the **entry emotion**; the page must still sell **operational** value or you attract people who want a checkbox, not a product. The arc: **mandate → your process is still messy → we structure the process.**

### Visitor mindset

- **Fear:** “We will be non-compliant or late.”
- **Confusion:** “KSeF is a technical thing; I don’t know how it connects to *my* approval flow.”
- **Risk for you:** Visitors want “a KSeF integration” and disappear when they see setup work. **Preempt** with “intake + match + policy” in the same block as KSeF.

### Page narrative

1. **Hero** — mandate is real; **scattered process** is the avoidable part.
2. **“What KSeF actually forces”** — 2–3 sentences in **plain language**; link to a longer blog for nerds. No jargon wall.
3. **“Where we sit in your stack”** — diagram: contractor invoice → *your* validation/match/approval → accounting/KSeF handoff. Honest about what is **yours** vs. partner/ERP.
4. **Capabilities** — numbered path; each step says *who* it helps (finance, contractor, ops).
5. **Proof** — date of readiness, any certification language **only** if accurate; “used in production for…”
6. **Qualification** — one question in demo form: “How many B2B contractor invoices per month?” to filter “checkbox” leads.

### Hero — draft copy block

- **Headline:** KSeF-ready contractor invoicing—without a second, disconnected workflow for your team.
- **Subhead:** Receive contractor invoices, align them to contracts and approvers, then push what’s cleared to your finance stack. Built for companies where **B2B contractors** are a real cost line, not a one-off e-invoicing project.
- **Primary CTA:** See how the workflow works  
- **Secondary:** Talk to us (if enterprise-heavy segment)

**Badge line (if true):** e.g. “KSeF-ready from [date] for [scope]—see product notes.”

### Section copy (deeper)

**“Why mandate ≠ a PDF”**

E-invoicing law doesn’t create **discipline** in matching, approvals, or payment timing—it creates a **format** and a **track**. The expensive failures are still operational: wrong entity, wrong period, paid without approver, contractor not in the system. Your copy should make that **obvious in one short paragraph**.

**Qualifying disclaimer (reduces bad leads)**

- If you are only looking for a minimal bill sender with no link to **contractor and approval policy**, we may not be the right fit; here’s our ICP. (Polite, confident.)

### Objections

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| We already have an accounting tool that does KSeF | Differentiate: **contractor** lifecycle, approvals, and **contract** context—not GL-first. |
| Is this only Poland? | Be explicit about current markets; **don’t** imply coverage you don’t have. |

### Sub-tests within C

- **Fear headline** (mandate / scattered data) vs **empowerment** (readiness + control).
- **Demo form:** optional free-text “What system posts to KSeF today?” to route sales and improve copy.

## Variant D — ICP callout (software houses & agencies)

### Strategy (summary)

The hero **filters**; the page must still work for a reader who is “agency-adjacent” (e.g. product company with heavy contractor use). Consider **dynamic** hero by UTM: same body, different hero for software vs. agency if both matter.

### Visitor mindset

- **Identified user:** “Finally something that’s not Workday and not a spreadsheet.”
- **Skepticism:** “This will be too generic; our billing is SOW / time-based / retainer.” You must show **familiarity** in subsection titles, not only the hero.

### Page narrative

1. **Hero** — segment in the H1; subhead clarifies *size* and *B2B* immediately.
2. **“If this is your world”** — 3–4 scenarios: rotating specialists, SOW changes, many small invoices, project codes on PDFs. Pick what’s true in your ICP.
3. **Map to product** — each scenario → one way the product creates a single object (contractor, engagement, document).
4. **Integrations** — only the ones that matter to this ICP (e.g. Jira, Linear, Google Workspace) without a zoo of logos.
5. **Proof** — testimonial *from the same segment* (even “beta, software house, 30 contractors” is better than a finance-only quote).
6. **Not for** — staff employees, EOR, global compliance packages—tighten positioning.

### Hero — draft copy block

- **Headline (software + agencies):** The contractor layer for software and creative teams: from contract to payment run, without a dozen tools.
- **Subhead:** Built for **5–50 B2B contractors**—onboarding, documents, incoming invoices, approvals, and offboarding—so finance and project leads see the same truth.
- **Primary CTA:** Start free trial  
- **Secondary:** How agencies use it (case scroll)

**Narrow ICP line (eyebrow):** For orgs 10–200 people; if you have two contractors, you’re not wrong—just not who we optimize for *yet*.

### Section copy (deeper)

**Scenarios (examples you can turn into 60–100 words each)**

- **SOW / statement of work changes mid-quarter:** the contract object updates; new invoices are checked against the **current** terms, not January’s Google Doc.
- **Project codes on invoices:** matching rules your finance can enforce before pay.
- **Offboarding at contract end:** access and documents aren’t a separate panicked Trello (tie to your real offboarding story).

### Objections

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| We use Deel for some people | “Great for that slice; we run **local B2B** where you don’t use EOR.” (No mud.) |
| Jira/PM tool is the source of truth for time | Position as **complement**: time in PM; **commercial truth** in contractor + contract + invoice. |

### Sub-tests

- **Headline:** “Software houses and agencies” vs **outcome** for same segment (speed to pay) without changing body.
- **Logos** above fold vs. one **detailed** quote below.

## Variant E — vs. alternatives (not EOR, not “another HRIS”)

### Strategy (summary)

This variant works when the visitor has **category confusion**. It shortens the sales call but can alienate **cold** traffic. Use for **retargeting**, comparison intent keywords, and “we looked at Deel/Remote” inbound.

### Visitor mindset

- **Question:** “Is this the same as X?”
- **Goal:** I want a **map**: where I put contractor ops, where I do not need you, where my existing tools stay.

### Page narrative

1. **Hero** — you are *not* the global employer; you *are* the B2B contractor loop in Europe at SMB scale.
2. **“When EOR is right, when it isn’t”** — 4–5 bullet decision guide (not a slam table). Honesty builds credit.
3. **Simple 3-column comparison** — You vs. EOR vs. “status quo (sheet + email).” Rows: *who you employ*, *invoicing model*, *approvals & pay*, *what you should expect to pay* (ranges or “request pricing,” not fake numbers for competitors).
4. **What we integrate with** — bank, accounting, e-invoicing, identity—**without** claiming to replace.
5. **CTA** — trial + “get the comparison one-pager” if legal prefers PDF.

### Hero — draft copy block

- **Headline:** Not a global employer platform. A contractor workflow for B2B relationships in the EU.
- **Subhead:** Local contracts, contractor records, incoming invoices, approvals, and payment runs—when you **don’t** need EOR for these people, but you **do** need a system.
- **Primary CTA:** See how it fits your stack  
- **Secondary:** EOR vs. this (scroll)

**Tone note:** Factual, not smug. Never name a competitor in the hero if legal/marketing dislikes it; “EOR” is a category, not a brand.

### Section copy (deeper)

**“When to use EOR (short)**  
Cross-border, employ-like risk transfer, you want one invoice from a platform—fine; that’s a different product.

**When B2B contractor ops is the job**  
They invoice you, you have a B2B agreement, you need **approvals, audit, and pay discipline** in your own entity. That’s the job we do.

**Comparison table (row ideas, not copy-ready)**

- **Primary user object:** contractor + engagement vs. employee in EOR country vs. rows in a sheet.
- **Invoicing:** B2B invoice to your entity vs. platform billing vs. chaos.
- **Your finance team’s job:** matching and approval in one system vs. depending on EOR for some vs. ad hoc.
- **Typical ICP size:** 5–50 contractors, single or few entities—*only if accurate.*

### Objections

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| You’re cheaper, so you must be worse | Reframe: **different job**; you’re not undercutting EOR—you’re *not* selling EOR. |
| We need a bit of both | “Hybrid stacks are normal; here’s the boundary of what we do.” |

### Sub-tests

- **Table above fold** (dense) vs **story first**, table at third screen.
- **“Not Deel”** in headline (strong, risky) vs **“Not EOR”** (safer, category-correct for many cases).

## Variant F — Security, audit, “source of record”

### Strategy (summary)

Sells to **board**, **head of finance**, and **IT/security** reviewers. Balance **controls** with **time-to-value** in section 2 or 3, or the page feels like GRC and kills PLG.

### Visitor mindset

- **Fear:** “We can’t prove who approved a payment in six months.”
- **Need:** RLS, access control, DPA, subprocessors, retention—*somewhere* they can link internally.

### Page narrative

1. **Hero** — every invoice **linked** to a contract and an approval *event* (not a vibe: system-enforced where true).
2. **“What ‘audit’ means in practice”** — 3 bullets: e.g. approver, timestamp, contract version, document trail—tied to **features you have**.
3. **Security (plain language)** — tenant isolation, least privilege, how secrets are handled at a **high** level; link to **security page** and **DPA**.
4. **Speed block** — “This doesn’t add two weeks to every pay run”—implementation narrative.
5. **Proof** — ISO/SOC *only* if you have it; else “security program” + willingness to share questionnaire.
6. **CTA** — “Book a security / workflow walkthrough” + trial for PLG.

### Hero — draft copy block

- **Headline:** Every contractor invoice, tied to a contract and a clear approver—before money leaves the account.
- **Subhead:** A single, permissioned system for your contractor and finance teams: approvals, document history, and a trail that doesn’t live in inboxes. Built for B2B contractors in the EU, at a scale where **trust** and **velocity** both matter.
- **Primary CTA:** Book a walkthrough  
- **Secondary:** Start free trial

### Section copy (deeper)

**“Audit in one paragraph”**  
Describe the **defensible** story: *invoice I received* → *contract it matched* → *people who had to say yes* → *what was included in a payment batch*. If any step is “aspirational,” don’t put it in writing.

**Security (don’t drown them)**

- Short bullets: data residency **if** relevant, encryption in transit, encryption at rest, tenant isolation, backup posture **as you can state truthfully**—link out for 5-page version.

### Objections

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| We need SOC 2 / ISO | If in progress, say **in progress** with rough timeline; if not, be honest and lean on DPA + architecture + customer references. |
| Too heavy, we need PLG | Dual CTA: security buyers get a call; teams get trial—**same** product, different motion. |

### Sub-tests

- **“Audit trail”** vs **“Who approved”** in headline (emotional vs. legal).
- **Security above fold** (badges) vs **only** in a strip before footer.

## Variant G — Product-led: the loop in one visual

### Strategy (summary)

Minimizes adjectives; **maximizes clarity**. Fails if the reader doesn’t self-identify—use a **one-line ICP** under the diagram.

### Visitor mindset

- **“Show me the path once.”** Technical and product-led buyers; fast bounce if generic.

### Page narrative

1. **Hero** — diagram 5–6 steps; H1 is short; ICP in one line under diagram.
2. **Click/expand** each step (optional) with 2–3 sentences + crop screenshot.
3. **“Not shown: your bank and accountant still do their job”** — one paragraph on boundaries.
4. **Integrations** as **icons in order** in the flow, not a grid of 20 logos.
5. **Loom** 60–90s in modal; transcript below for skimmers.
6. **CTA** — trial + watch video.

### Hero — draft copy block

- **Headline:** From contract to payment run—in one system for your B2B contractors.
- **Subhead (under diagram, not replacing it):** Onboarding, documents, invoices, approvals, and offboarding for teams with **5–50 contractors** in the EU. Not employee payroll. Not EOR.
- **Primary CTA:** Start free trial  
- **Secondary:** Watch 90-second overview

**Diagram step labels (example—align to your product):**  
Contractor & contract → Documents & policy → **Incoming invoice** → **Approval** → **Payment run** → Offboarding / access.

### Objections (compact)

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Looks like a lot of steps | “Each step is one place your team *already* does manually— here it’s **connected**.” |
| We only need invoice | *If true:* point to a lighter path or say “our wedge is the full object”—honesty. |

### Sub-tests

- **Animated** vs **static** diagram; **5 steps** vs **7** (detail vs. clarity).
- **Video** vs no video (watch rate vs. direct trial).

## Shared: objection bank (any variant)

Use these in FAQ or a single “**Common questions**” block; reorder by variant (F puts security up; C puts KSeF up).

- **“We already use [accounting/ERP name].”**  
  *Angle:* You don’t replace the ledger; you **front** the contractor process so what hits the books matches what was approved and contracted.

- **“Our contractors are in many countries.”**  
  *Angle:* B2B and **scope** of markets you support, honestly; EOR is a different link for employ-like relationships.

- **“How long to go live?”**  
  *Angle:* Phased: first records → first invoice in workflow → first pay run; no fake “hours” unless true.

- **“What about legal/tax advice?”**  
  *Angle:* Operational workflows and record-keeping; **not** a substitute for counsel or your accountant. (Match your `CLAUDE.md` / legal stance.)

- **“Another subscription?”**  
  *Angle:* Cost of a wrong pay run / audit prep / finance overtime—only if you can speak credibly; otherwise stay with **time** and **risk** in soft terms.

## Shared: FAQ (copy-paste starters; legal review)

1. **Who is this for?**  
   Companies in [markets you serve] with roughly **5–50 B2B contractors** and internal finance/ops—**not** full employee payroll, **not** global EOR for those contractors.

2. **Do you replace our accountant or ERP?**  
   No. We coordinate the **contractor** lifecycle and approvals; your accounting system remains the system of record for the books *as you define it*.

3. **How does e-invoicing / KSeF fit in?**  
   [Factual, narrow sentence about your product’s role + link to help doc.]

4. **Where is data stored?**  
   High level + link to DPA / security page.

5. **Can we get a DPA / ISO / SOC?**  
   As appropriate to your real posture.

## Suggested A/B program (unchanged logic)

1. **Phase 1** — A vs. B vs. C (same page skeleton; different hero + first 2 blocks).  
2. **Phase 2** — Winner vs. **demo-primary** CTA in selected markets.  
3. **Phase 3** — Dedicated **D** (segment) vs. winner—measure **SQL quality**.  
4. **Phase 4** — **E** and **F** on retargeting and intent channels, not always cold.

## Copy guardrails (all variants)

- **Avoid** implied legal or tax advice; “operational compliance” and “your counsel / accountant” where needed.
- **Align** every concrete claim with the live product; screenshots in the section they belong to.
- **One job per hero**; deeper sections can add nuance.
